# Employee Exit Employer Brand Crisis

Use this skill to handle employee-exit and employer-brand public opinion with a PR judgment mindset: first identify the real risk, then prioritize actions, then write restrained and usable messaging.

## Core Judgment

Treat former-employee posts as more than “one complaint.” They can become:

- An employee relations issue externalized into public view.
- An employer brand trust issue.
- A management governance issue if other employees join.
- A regulated-institution credibility issue when the organization is a bank, fintech, consumer finance company, or other financial institution.

Default posture:

1. Do not start with deletion, legal threats, or official statements.
2. Start with facts, evidence risk, spread pattern, and emotional resonance.
3. Avoid any action that can be screenshotted as pressure, retaliation, astroturfing, or suppression.
4. In financial contexts, keep statements restrained, factual, and compliance-reviewable.

## Workflow

### 1. Restate the Case

Briefly restate:

- Organization type and user role.
- Employee background and influence base.
- Initial platform, content, timing, and engagement.
- Spread platforms and comment ecology.
- Internal actions already taken.
- Objectives, red lines, and unknowns.

Then give an initial rating, usually one of:

- **Level 3: Monitor** - single platform, low interaction, no employee relay.
- **Level 2: Handle** - cross-platform spread, comment resonance, former-employee relay, media pickup risk.
- **Level 1: Institutional crisis** - mainstream media, strong evidence, multiple employees, labor/legal/regulatory/customer/data-security expansion.

### 2. Judge the Real Risk

Prioritize these questions:

1. **Spread**: Is the issue still local, or has it crossed platform and community boundaries?
2. **Emotion**: Are comments forming a shared “this happened to me too” narrative?
3. **Content**: Does it involve overtime, performance, layoffs, discrimination, bullying, manager speech, compensation, or compliance?
4. **Evidence**: Could the employee release screenshots, recordings, attendance records, performance records, emails, or customer/business-sensitive material?
5. **Business context**: Could financial media, regulators, partners, recruitment candidates, or internal employees reinterpret the issue as governance failure?

For financial institutions, add one filter:

- Does any narrative imply operational disorder, compliance weakness, customer rights issues, data leakage, product marketing irregularity, or regulatory sensitivity? If yes, escalate legal/compliance immediately.

### 3. Build the 72-Hour Response

Use the sequence:

```text
snapshot -> stop risky actions -> verify facts -> assess evidence -> align internal owners -> contact employee softly -> prepare media holding -> monitor trend -> decide response level
```

Never make “water army,” mass reporting, attacks on the employee, or forced deletion the main strategy. If such actions already happened, recommend stopping visible or adversarial versions immediately.

### 4. Decide Whether to Respond

Prefer no official statement if:

- The issue is still platform-local.
- No strong evidence is released.
- No mainstream financial media inquiry exists.
- A statement would amplify the post.

Prepare but do not publish a holding statement if:

- Financial media may ask.
- Industry groups are circulating screenshots.
- The employee may release more evidence.

Consider public response only if:

- Media inquiry is unavoidable.
- Strong evidence is viral.
- Multiple employees relay the same claim.
- The issue has already become a named organizational trust issue.

## Messaging Rules

Use this tone:

- Concerned, restrained, factual, non-defensive.
- Respect employee lawful expression.
- Say facts are being verified.
- Protect personal privacy.
- Commit to review if internal management issues exist.

Avoid:

- “The claims are all false.”
- “Malicious smearing.”
- “We reserve the right to pursue legal responsibility.”
- “The employee had poor performance.”
- “The company has always valued employees.”
- “Please delete the post.”
- “This affects the company’s reputation.”
- Any wording that hints at career consequences.

## Output Style

Lead with judgment, not a generic checklist. Give priority order and tradeoffs. Mark unknowns that materially affect the judgment. For high-stakes financial or legal claims, state that legal/compliance confirmation is required and avoid inventing facts.
